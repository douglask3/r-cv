Please provide details of your PhD or equivalent qualifications and experience in Geography, Biology, Environmental Sciences, or related subject.

I completed my PhD in Climate Change Ecology just over a year ago (thesis available here: http://douglask3.github.io/docs/thesis.pdf) where I utilised remote sensed and ground-based GIS observations and experimental data to develop, constrain and evaluate Dynamic Global Vegetation Models (DGVMs) Prentice2011; Kelley2013; kaminski2013bethy; Hantson. I used one of these models, the LPX-DGVM Kelley2014a, to assess ecosystem resilience to disturbance regimes Kelley2014a; Zeppel2014 and to project the impact of future climate change on ecosystem composition, processes and services in Australia. For my thesis, this focussed on changes in vegetation-modulated future fire regimes and their associated impacts kelley2014modelling; Harrisonc, but I have since used the model to explore potential changes in future ecosystem resilience, carbon stocks Kelley2014b and water resources Ukkola. My thesis was well received by my examiners, who consistently stated that it fell into the “top 10%” they've ever assessed. The major theme of my MSc in Earth Systems Science was linking earth system processes to climate policy, mainly via ecosystem service valuation - both through assigning a monetary value, similar to costanza1997value, or through other metrics such as biodiversity or carbon storage Kelley2008. I found this the most fascinating aspect of the masters programme, as it provided a direct link between ecological science and social policy, and it had a major influence on the main aims of my PhD.

Please provide information to evidence your experience in using GIS (i.e ArcGIS) and statistical software (i.e. R) to analyse large geospatial datasets.

During my PhD, I developed an R-based benchmarking system which evaluates spatial and temporal patterns of multiple processes of simulated by vegetation models Kelley2013. The system uses specially designed metric to quantify "distances" between simulations and global remote-sensed observational datasets, and formed an essential part of model development in my thesis. Processing of the observational datasets included resampling high spatial and temporal resolution RAW satellite data onto coarser resolution model grid, applying land/sea/ice masks, and converting different file types (HDF, ASCII, tiff, img etc.) to a standard format (NetCDF) using raster, sp, maptools and rgeos packages in R. I was a lead collaborator on the construction of a site-based database designed to determine climatic and wildfire controls on the prevalence of plant resprouting ability (RS) kelley2014modelling; Harrisonc. I used R to extract data points from remote-sensed and climate observations, and ArcGIS to extract vegetation and soil information from vector datasets (the National Vegetation Information system and ASRIS Atlas of Australian Soils) for each of these sites. I selected possible control variables after testing for redundancy using principal component analysis and quantified the influence of each variable using a Generalised Linear Model (GLM) in R. This analysis demonstrated the importance RS as a recovery trait to specific fire regimes and was used to guide the incorporation of post-fire resprouting into LPX-DGVM Kelley2014a; Harrison. It also showed a high abundance of specific RS types associated with areas of highly variable rainfall, which has spawned research into RS as a recovery adaptation to drought Zeppel2014. In my thesis introduction, I again used a GLM in R to construct a simple statistical fire model describing the maximum expected burnt area for three key components of fire: fuel availability, fuel moisture, and ignitions. I used each components contribution and residuals to construct a map of the limitations of fire in Australia (Figure 1.6 - kelley2014modelling). I have also used ArcGIS to construct maps of water-holding capacity, percolation rates and thermal diffusivity of soils based on the ASRIS maps in order to guide the development of below-ground processes in LPX. I have since repeated this analysis using QGIS - an open source GIS software product.

Please provide details of your experience (if any) of using remote sensing software (e.g. ENVI)

I developed a new lightning-fire start model during my PhD Kelley2014a by comparing remote-sensed LIS lightning flash count data to point-based, ground lightning strike observations in order to determine if individual flashes were inter-cloud or cloud to ground. This involved determining location of strikes in satellite data and matching point-based ground observations using Matlab's HDF package. I have since repeated this analysis using raster and GIS packages in R. In addition, during a course on Natural Hazards as part of my MSc in Earth Systems Science, I analysed movement and concentrations of volcanic ash clouds with multi-spectrum, split-window technique's using ENVI/IDL programming. I also used ENVI to detect and remove unwanted pixels (i.e, water and cloud), and to construct multi-band fire indices from MODIS burnt area products as part of my masters dissertation Kelley2008 .
