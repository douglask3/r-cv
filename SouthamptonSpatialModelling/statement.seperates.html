Please provide information to evidence your experience in spatial and/or ecological modelling.

I have been the lead developer of the LPX-DGVM for that last 6 years Prentice2011; Kelley2014a. This model is driven with spatially distributed monthly climate, land use and soils inputs as well as atompsheric CO2 concentrations in order to simulate terrestial bioshphere processes. Vegetation is split along descrete plant functioanl types (PFTS). These PFTS establish if climate is within their tolarated ranges, grow, compete with other established PFTs for resources, and are killed via light competition, carbon limitation, and disturbance (e.g. fire). The mix of PFTs found in each location allows assessment of broad vegetation types or biome Sitch2003. The model also simulates vegetation productivity, vegetation-driven carbon cycle, the impact of vegetation and climate on fire and the water cycle abnd many more processes. Most of my work with LPX has foccussed on development of the fire disturbance component and fire-vegetation interactions. This has involved parameterization of phyical processes such as incorporaton of a lighnting ignition algorithm and fuel drying times Kelley2014a, and ecological processes such as vegetation fuel production, fuel decomponsition, and modelling plant traits to for fire protection (such as evolutionary driven changes in bark thickness) and recovery (such as competation between rapidly estatilishing plants and resprouting plants) Kelley2014a; Harrisonc. As well as development of the fire component, I have also worked on competition between grass PFTs and plant root profiles in order to improve representation of vegetation in semi-arid ecosystem. Improvment of the model allowed me to assess and seperate climate and vegetation interactions with fire and hydrology. This included counter-intuitive but important results that policymakers need to take into account, such as: CO2-related increases in vegetation may have added pressure on available water supply in already dry and drying regions, further compounding predicted drought Ukkola; and that changes in the likelihood of fire events is dependent on the complex interactions between climate amd vegetation composition/productivity - not just climate by itself Kelley2014b; Harrisonc. I have experiance developing and using other spatial ecological models besides LPX (and its pre-curser, LPJ-GLOBFIRM: Sitch2003; Kelley2013). I have coded and driven the Simple Diognostic Biosphere Model (SDBM), which calculates productivity of and water use by vegetation based on simple relationships with remote-sensed vegetative land-surface propertises. I used this model as an evaulation tool of carbon and water-dynamics in more complex terrestial bioshophere models, as a away off asseing the impact of vegetation-parameter choice on ecosystem productivity Kelley2013, and to assess the affects of vegetetaion productivity and fuel moisture limitation on burnt area kelley2014modelling. I have also worked on the vegetation model (BETHY) in the Carbon Cycle Data Assimilation System (CCDAS) which calculates likely values of key ecologicl and Earth System model parameters based on piro estimates and model optimization against spatially-distributed remote sensed land-surface and site based carbon flux obsverations Kelley2008; kaminski2013bethy. Recently, I have been involved in projects exploreing plant allocation and phenology stratagies using a simple, site-based ecological model (GDAY: <>).

Please provide information to evidence your expertise in advanced statistical analyses, ideally of spatial datasets.

As a major part of this thesis, I developed a comprehensive benchmarking system which allows evaluation of spatial and temporal patterns of multiple aspects of the simulated vegetation, hydrology, disturbance regimes and ecosystem fluxes in vegetation models Kelley2013. This system uses multiple spatial and temporal datasets - many of which I created from romote sensed observations - and applies statistical metrics (normalised mean (squared) error, taxi-cab and square chord distances plus metrics I designed myself to assess temporal performance) to quantify the "distance" between simulated and obsveraved variables. These metrics are normalised in such away as to allow comparison between model processes. I also designed two 'null' models to set benchmarks of minimum experected performance which aids interpretation of metrioc scores. This benchmarking system serves several purposes: (1) it quantifies how well a model performs across a comprehensive range of important processes; (2) it allows the identification of model weaknesses through comparison of the performance with respect to different benchmarks and thus facilitates the identification of processes that require improvement; (3) it quantifies the differences between model versions, which allows assessment of the overall impact of new parameterizations. Application of this benchmarking system to LPX identified key geographic areas and prcoesses of model weakness and formed the basis of model devlopment throughout the rest of my thesis. I have since made the metrics and null models into R-package a freely avaiable for anyone to download and use Kelley2015. The system has also been adopted by other modelling groups and is being used by a new Model Intercomparison Project to assess the impact of different fire modelling approaches Hantson, and to help guide the developmental direction of the fire-vegetation modelling community Hantson2016. I was a lead collabrotator on a project that aimed to determine climate and disturbance controls on prevelance of plant resprouting ability (RS) and type kelley2014modelling; Harrisonc. This involved the creation of a database of site-based species inventories to determine indicies of RS abuncance and species richness. These indicies of abundance where compared against climate and disturbance variables which selcted after assessing co-correlation between variables using rundancy analysis. I used a Generalised Linear Model (GLM) approuch to indetify and quantify variables that control RS. This analysis demstrated the importance RS as a recovcery trait to intermediate-intensity and resonably frequant (10 - 100 years) fires, and was used to guide and evaluate incorpration of post-fire reprouting into LPX-DGVM Kelley2014a; Harrison. It also showed high abundance of specific types of RS in areas of highly variable and unreliable rainfall, which has spawning research into RS as a recovery adpatation to drought Zeppel2014. In the introduction of my thesis, I again used the GLM approuch with a simple statistical fire-limitation model I developed to described the maximum expected byurnt area for three key components of fire: plant productivity (proxy for fuel load); aridity (for fuel moisture); and lightning (ignitions). I used the GLM to determine components and residuals of each of the three variables in order to map the contribution of each limitation (i.e, too little fuel, too wet, or too little ignitions) to the control of fire in Australia (Figure 1.6 - kelley2014modelling).
