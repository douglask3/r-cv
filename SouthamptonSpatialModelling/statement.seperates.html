Please provide information to evidence your experience in spatial and/or ecological modelling.

I have been the lead developer of the LPX-DGVM for that last 6 years Prentice2011; Kelley2014a. This model is driven with spatially distributed monthly climate, land use and soils inputs as well as atompsheric CO2 concentrations in order to simulate terrestial bioshphere processes. Vegetation is split into plant functioanl types (PFTS). These PFTS establish if climate is within their tolarated ranges, grow, compete with other established PFTs for resources, and are killed via light competition, carbon limitation, and disturbance. The mix of PFTs found in each location allows assessment of broad vegetation types Sitch2003. The model also simulates vegetation productivity, and the impact of vegetation and climate carbon and water cycles, fire and many more processes. Most of my work with LPX has foccussed on development of fire-vegetation interactions such as fuel production, fuel decomponsition, and incorporating plant traits to for fire protection (e.g. evolutionary driven changes in bark thickness) and recovery (such as competation between rapidly estatilishing plants and resprouting plants) Kelley2014a; Harrisonc. I have also worked on modelling plant root profiles and grass PFT competition in order to improve representation of vegetation in semi-arid ecosystem. The improved model allowed me to seperate climate and vegetation influances on fire and hydrology. This included counter-intuitive but important results that policymakers need to take into account, such as: changes in the likelihood of fire events is dependent on the complex interactions between climate and vegetation composition/productivity - not just climate by itself Kelley2014b; Harrisonc; and that CO2-related increases in vegetation may have added pressure on available water supply in already dry and drying regions, further compounding predicted drought Ukkola. I have experiance developing and using other spatial ecological models besides LPX. I have coded and driven the Simple Diognostic Biosphere Model (SDBM), which calculates productivity of and water use by vegetation based on simple relationships with remote-sensed vegetative land-surface propertises. I used this model as an evaulation tool of carbon and water-dynamics in complex terrestial bioshophere models; as a away of asseing the impact of vegetation-parameter choice on ecosystem productivity Kelley2013, and to assess the affects of vegetetaion productivity and fuel moisture limitation on burnt area kelley2014modelling. I have also worked on the vegetation model in the Carbon Cycle Data Assimilation System, which calculates likely values of key ecologicl and Earth System model parameters based on piro estimates and model optimization against spatially-distributed remote sensed land-surface and site based carbon flux obsverations Kelley2008; kaminski2013bethy. Recently, I have been involved in projects exploreing plant allocation and phenology stratagies using a simple, site-based ecological model (GDAY: <>).

Please provide information to evidence your expertise in advanced statistical analyses, ideally of spatial datasets.

As part of my thesis, I developed a comprehensive benchmarking system which allows evaluation of spatial and temporal patterns of multiple aspects of simulated vegetation, hydrology, disturbance regimes and ecosystem fluxes in vegetation models Kelley2013. This system uses multiple datasets and applies statistical metrics (including normalised mean error, taxi-cab and metrics I designed myself) to quantify "distances" between simulated and obsveraved variables. These metrics are normalised in such away as to allow comparison between model processes. I also designed two 'null' models to set benchmarks for aiding interpretation of metric scores. Application of this benchmarking system to LPX identified key geographic areas and processes of model weakness which formed the basis of model development throughout the rest of my thesis, and was used to assess the impact of this model development on performance. I have since made the metrics and null models into R-package Kelley2015. The system has also been adopted by other modelling groups and is being used by a new Model Intercomparison Project to assess the impact of different fire modelling approaches Hantson, and to help guide the developmental direction of the fire-vegetation modelling community Hantson2016. I was a lead collabrotator on a project that aimed to determine climate and disturbance controls on prevelance of plant resprouting ability (RS) kelley2014modelling; Harrisonc. This involved the creation of a database of site-based species inventories to construct RS abuncance and species richness indicies. These indicies where compared against climate and disturbance variables which were selcted after testing for co-correlation using rundancy analysis. I used a Generalised Linear Model (GLM) approuch to indetify variables that control RS. This analysis demstrated the importance RS as a recovcery trait to specific fire-regimes, and was used to guide incorpration of post-fire reprouting into LPX-DGVM Kelley2014a; Harrison. It also showed high abundance of specific types of RS in areas of highly variable rainfall, which has spawning research into RS as a recovery adpatation to drought Zeppel2014. In the introduction of my thesis, I again used a GLM to construct a simple statistical fire-limitation model describing the maximum expected burnt area for three key components of fire: fuel avavilablity, fuel moisture and ignitions. I used each components contribution and residuals to construct a map of the limitations of fire in Australia (Figure 1.6 - kelley2014modelling).
