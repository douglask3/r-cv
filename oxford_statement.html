I have recently completed a PhD in climate change ecology, where I developed a complex numerical vegetation model to explore the impacts of future climate change in a moisture controlled tropical region similar to East Africa: Australia. Many of the climate controls and impacts are shared across Australia and East Africa: both have transitions from wet to dry tropical and sub-tropical ecosystems, where natural and agricultural productivity is particularly sensitive to available moisture (Seddon et al. 2016); both are prone to water-related natural disasters such as drought, flooding and fire (Lehmann et al. 2011; Ward et al. 2014); and both are exposed to highly uncertain but potentially devastating climate change, whereby changes in precipitation, increases in evaporation, and increases in extreme precipitation events may lead to more flooding and drought (Scholze et al. 2006; Jiménez Cisneros et al. 2014; Settele et al. 2014). However, the impacts of climate change on water security in East Africa, and indeed most of the poorer regions of the world, are likely to be much worse than for Australia (Grey & Sadoff, 2007; Vorosmarty et al. 2010). The rural poor, who make up roughly 80% of the regions population (The Would Bank Group, 2016) are already severely affected by periodic droughts and floods (Carter et al. 2007). The rapid economic development in some East African countries (IMF, 2012) may, if unguided, put additional strain on water resources, as has happened in other parts of the world where increased urbanisation and has led to increased water demand from water-intensive agriculture and energy productionm and decreased supply because of pollution and changes in land use (Falkenmark & Widstrand 1992). One way to mitigate possible harmful effects of climate change on water security is continued development and implementation of “early warning systems” which allow countries to predict and prepare for future drought events or flood-prone seasons (Washington & Downing, 1999; Washington et al. 2006). These systems often take advantage of teleconnections with preceding climate indicators such as ocean temperatures/temperature gradients (e.g. ENSO, AMO, Indian Ocean Indices, shown to be particularly useful in East and Southern Africa; Todd & Washington, 2004; Mohino et al. 2011). However, these connections, and their resultant impacts, are likely to change with climate (Müller & Roeckner 2006; Stevenson 2011; Cai et al. 2014) and land cover (Sitch et al. 2008; Harrison & Kelley submitted). This is perhaps of particular concern in East Africa where, for example, the transition from drought-prone north to flood-prone equatorial regions during EL Nino years has already proven difficult to predict for even the current El Nino event (Bonifacio, 2015). Climate Science Research Portfolio

I completed my PhD in Climate Change Ecology just over a year ago. My thesis (“Modelling Australian Fire Regimes”, Kelley 2014), and subsequent postdoctoral research, focussed on climate dynamics in Australia, exploring the moisture controls on vegetation productivity and vegetation-fire interactions in tropical and subtropical wet and dry ecosystems – similar climate zones as those experienced in East Africa. It used satellite and ground-based observations of weather, climate and vegetation properties to develop and evaluate complex numerical vegetation models (Prentice et al. 2011; Kelley et al. 2014; Kelley et al. in prep; Stijn et al in prep). I have driven some of these models with historical/reanalysis climate data to assess how teleconnections between ENSO and climate affect fire (Kelley, 2008; Prentice et al. 2011). I used the model I developed as part of my thesis – the LPX-Mv1 vegetation model (Kelley et al. 2014) - to assess the impact of future climate change on ecosystem resilience and the carbon cycle (Kelley & Harrison 2014) fire-dynamics (Harrison & Kelley, submitted) and hydrology (Ukkola et al. submitted). For these future climate experiments, I developed an “anomalies procedure” to derive inputs for LPX-Mv1 from CMIP5 future climate model data (Kelley & Harrison 2014) and to assess changes in climate variability from present day climate. My thesis demonstrated the importance of incorporating climate-vegetation-fire interactions into present and future climate impact assessments, with my improved dry-climate vegetation simulations altering the magnitude, and in some cases the sign, of predicted future vegetation resilience and water availability. This included counterintuitive but important results that policymakers need to take into account, such as: CO2-related increases in vegetation may have added pressure on available water supply in already dry and drying parts of Australia (Ukkola et al. 2014); and that the main driver for changes in fire, and therefore fire-related impacts, will be almost entirely controlled by changes in vegetation cover rather than weather and climate (Kelley & Harrison 2014; Harrison & Kelley submitted). My thesis examiners consistently stated my thesis fell into the “top 10%” they've ever assessed. I have also used and developed simple vegetation models, as well as a simple climate box-model as part of my masters, in Python, R and Matlab (Kelley et al. 2013; Kelley et al. in prep; Whitley et al. in prep), and have performed statistical analysis and plotting in Python, R, Matlab and, during my masters, in IDL. I have developed some of my R-code into software packages, now available for anyone to download and use (Kelley 2015; Kelley & Whitley 2016 – both available from http://douglask3.github.io/tools), one of which has recently been adopted and applied to a new vegetation model inter-comparison project (fireMIP: Stijn et al. submitted & in prep). My work on developing and evaluating vegetation models has involved processing a range of GIS products, from raw satellite data (e.g. the LIS/OTD flash counts in Kelley et al. 2014; MODIS burnt area products in Kelley 2008 & Kelley et al. 2014) to processed satellite data (e.g. GFED burnt area in Prentice et al. 2011; Kelley et al. 2013 & 2014; SeaWifs vegetation absorbed radiation in Kelley et al. 2013) and vector ground observation (lightning counts and fire observations in Kelley et al. 2014). Processing these products has involved using a range of GIS software including ArcGIS and GRASS GIS (an open source equivalent), as well as GIS packages in R and Matlab.
