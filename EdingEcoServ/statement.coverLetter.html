Academic attainment

My research to date has focussed on constraining vegetation models to improve our undersatnding of the linkages between ecosystem dynamics, climate, land propertise and use in Australian tropical and subtropical woodland and savanna - similar vegetation types as those experienced in Mozambique. My PhD thesis in Climate Change Ecology focussed on the development of one of these model, LPX-Mv1, which I used to assess the impacts of future envoiromental change on climate,vegetation,fire and hydrology interactions. This included counterintuitive but important results for developing mitigation and adpation policy, such as: CO2-related increases in vegetation may have added pressure on available water supply in already dry and drying argicultural regions; and that the main driver for changes in fire and its impacts, will be almost entirely controlled by changes in vegetation/land cover rather than weather and climate. My thesis therefore tied together all major components of research that will be performed by the role by exploring the impact of ecosystem and land use change on society. The imporantce on my thesis was refelcted in my examiners comments, who consistently stated my thesis fell into the “top 10%” they've ever assessed.

Programming and Data analysis

My work on constraining vegetetaion models involved processing and staistical analysi of a range of ecological, climate and land use data, including remote sensed and ground-observed GIS data products and site-based observations. For example, development of the LPX-Mv1 model involved processesing RAW satalite and ground observed lighting data to design its fire ignition scheme; meta-data anaysis of soil maps root profiles to parameterize plant rooting depth; weather-station based atomospheric drying potential and site based production/decompoitsion flux inventories to develope a litter drying and decompoisition scheme; comparison of agricultural maps against fire observations to develope a staistical alorgirthm describing anthropagenic fire supression; and construction of two site-based plant trait databases describing woody plant adaptations to resist and recovery from disturbances (i.e bark thickness, resprouting and re-seeding stratagies). I publiched description of model development and its statistical analysis in Prentice2011;Kelley2014a and Kelley2014b.
