Academic attainment

My research to date has focussed on constraining vegetation models to improve our understanding of the linkages between ecosystem dynamics, climate, land properties and use in Australian tropical and subtropical woodland and savanna. My PhD thesis in Climate Change Ecology focussed on the development of one of these model, LPX, which I used to assess the impacts of future environmental change on climate, vegetation, fire and hydrology interactions. This included counterintuitive but important results important for developing mitigation and adaptation policy, such as: CO2-related increases in vegetation may have added pressure on available water supply in already dry and drying agricultural regions; and that the main driver for changes in fire and its impacts will be almost entirely controlled by changes in vegetation and land cover rather than weather and climate. My thesis therefore tied together all major components of research that will be performed by the role by exploring the impact of ecosystem and land use change on society. The importance on my thesis was reflected in my examiner's comments, who consistently stated my thesis fell into the “top 10%” they've ever assessed.

Programming and Data analysis

My work on constraining vegetation models involved the processing and statistical analysis of a range of ecological, climate and land use data, including remote sensed and ground-observed GIS data products and site-based observations. For example, development of the LPX model involved processing raw satellite and ground observed lighting data to design its fire ignitions scheme; soils maps and meta-data analysis of root profiles to parameterize plant rooting depth; weather-station based atmospheric drying potential and site based litter production/decomposition flux inventories to develop a litter drying and decomposition scheme; comparison of agricultural maps against fire observations to develop a statistical algorithm describing anthropogenic fire suppression; and construction of two site-based plant trait databases describing woody plant adaptations to resist and recovery from disturbances (i.e bark thickness, resprouting and re-seeding stratagies). The description of model development and its statistical analysis has been published in a number of peer-reviewed papers including Prentice2011;Kelley2014a and Kelley2014b. This demonstrates my invalable expertise in analysis of a variety of relevant ecological and land use data, and applying results to construct complex ecological models that can be used to assess impacts of environmental change. The trait database exploring plant disturbance recovery provides an example of my use of statistical knowledge and data management to tease apart complex relationships between local environmental conditions and vegetation trait expression. Here, I determined climatic and fire regime controls on the prevalence of plant resprouting ability (RS). I selected possible control variables after testing for redundancy using principal component analysis and quantified the degree of control of each component using a generalised linear model. This analysis demonstrated the importance RS as a recovery trait to specific fire regimes which was used to guide the incorporation of post-fire resprouting into LPX. It also showed a high abundance of specific RS types associated with areas of highly variable rainfall, which has spawned research into RS as a recovery adaptation to drought (see e.g. Zeppel2014). Performing these data analyses and statistical comparisons has allowed me to become fluent in most common statistical programming languages and GIS software appropriate for the role, including R, Python, Matlab, and IDL, ArcGIS, ENVI and QGIS. I have even developed some of my statistical R code into freely available software packages (http://douglask3.github.io/tools, see pg 7 of cv for more information). One of these packages (model benchmark metrics: http://douglask3.github.io/vegetation-model-benchmarking.html) has recently been adopted and applied to a new model inter-comparison project (fireMIP: Hantson2016; Hantson).
