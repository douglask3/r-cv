I have recently completed a PhD in climate change, where I utilised experimental and satellite data to developed and constrain a complex numerical vegetation model Kelley2014a. I used this model to explore the impacts of future climate change and climate extremes in Australia, including changes in fire Kelley2014b; Harrison, drought and hydrology Zeppel2014; Ukkola. Many of these predicted impacts have important consequences for mitigation Kelley2014b and adaptation Ukkola; Harrison policies. However, these impact "forcasts" still remain largely qualitative due uncertainties in anthropogenic forcing, in climate variability and their impact on localised weather extremes (e.g. Friedlingstein2006; robson2010understanding; Harrison). Reducing near-term uncertainty (i.e seasonal inter-annual and decadal) through better constrain internal climate system variability hawkins2009potential, would be a huge step forward in qualitative prediction of extreme events, and would help provide "early warning" seasonal forecasts and would aid prediction of decade variability in extreme longer-term planning for decadle changes. However, this requires attribution of extreme events between different anthropogenic forcing, inherent climate variability, or indeed changes in climate variability mediated by anthropogenic forcing, an extreme complex and difficult task hartmann2013observations. The East Asian Summer Monsoon (EASM) provides a great example of this, where weakening of the EASM in recent decades, which has been responsible for flooding in southern Asia and droughts in the North, has been attributed to a mix of drivers including global change from anthropogenic greenhouse gas emission hartmann2013observations, increases in aerosol emission associated from pollution and land use change Kaufman2002; Hsu2006; GRL:GRL22181; lau2006asian and changes in sea surface temperatures (SSTs), associated with the Pacific Decadal Variability and Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation Chang2000; hartmann2013observations. Several new climate model tools, such as GloSea5 (seasonal QJ:QJ2396) and DePreSys (inter-annual and decadale, smith2007objective), exploit low-frequency ocean oscillations, predictable for years to decades in advance boer2000study; boer2004long; boer2008multi; robson2010understanding. Recent hindcasts during specific ocean warming events using these tools has shownpromising results for near-term predictability of terrestrial climate variability (e.g. Liu2012b; Robson2013; Knight2014 for DePreSys). They would therefore be ideal for exploring teleconnections between preceding ocean variability and extreme climate events such as East Asian drought. I would relish the opportunity to use the experience I gained from studying climate change in Australia to work in a team that can help disentangle the drivers of extreme weather events. I believe EASM is an ideal case study for this, not just because the complex interactions between anthropogenic drivers and inherent climate variability provides such an interesting challenge to tease apart, but because any improvement in our predictive understanding of the monsoonal system will directly benefit ataptation policies that could help the 1/3 of the worlds population that rely on monsoonal rain for their water security.
